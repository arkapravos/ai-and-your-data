What Is Your Favorite AI Doing with Your Data?

And is it really any different from the software you have been trusting for years? I went looking for the answer. It was not what I expected.

AS
Arka Sarkar, PhD
Assistant Professor of Marketing, Sawyer Business School, Suffolk University

There is a question that keeps coming up in conversations about AI, and it sounds like this: Is AI learning everything about me? Are these companies harvesting my conversations to train their models?

It is a fair question. It deserves a real answer, not a hand-wave. So I decided to do something that, as far as I can tell, not enough people have done. I went through the actual privacy policies, compliance certifications, documented incidents, and public disclosures of every major AI lab. And then I did the same thing for the traditional software that most of us use every day without a second thought. Microsoft Office. Dropbox. Oracle. The tools that hold our data, our documents, our research, and our personal files.

The comparison is where the story gets interesting. Because the picture that emerges is more nuanced, more surprising, and in some places more uncomfortable than what people tend to assume about either side.

Here is what I found.

01 / The Policy Landscape
What the AI Labs Actually Do
The answer depends on which product you use

Every major AI company today operates on what I call a two-tier model. On the consumer side (the free version of ChatGPT, the standard Gemini app, free-tier Copilot), your conversations may be used to train future models. On the business and enterprise side (API access, enterprise accounts, paid team plans), your data is contractually off-limits for training. Full stop.

That distinction matters enormously, and most people do not realize it exists.

Let me walk through each company.

OpenAI (ChatGPT)

If you are on the Free, Plus, Go, or Pro plan, OpenAI uses your conversations to improve its models by default. Their own documentation states it plainly. But there is an opt-out toggle in Settings, a privacy portal at privacy.openai.com, and a Temporary Chat mode that keeps nothing.

Here is a wrinkle most people miss, though. Even with training turned off, if you click the thumbs-up or thumbs-down button on any response, that feedback can pull the entire conversation back into the training pipeline. It is buried in the help docs. It is not something most users would ever think to look for. And it is exactly the kind of detail that feeds legitimate distrust.

For Enterprise, Business, and API customers? No training. Period. That policy has been in place since March 2023, and Zero Data Retention options are available for organizations that want even tighter controls.

Anthropic (Claude)

Anthropic had the most privacy-forward stance of any lab for a long time. Until August 2025, they did not train on any consumer conversations. Then they introduced a choice: users now see a toggle in Settings that says "Improve Claude for everyone." You pick. Incognito chats are never used for training regardless, and deleted conversations are always excluded.

On the commercial side, Team, Enterprise, and API data is never used for training. API data retention was cut from 30 days to 7 in September 2025.

Google (Gemini)

Google is the most complicated of the four. Consumer Gemini trains on your conversations by default when "Keep Activity" is on, which it is for most users. And here is the part that gave me pause: conversations that are reviewed by human reviewers can be retained for up to three years, even if you delete your activity afterward.

That is a significant asterisk.

Paying for Gemini Advanced does not change this, which is different from Google's developer API (where the paid tier does exclude training). For Workspace and Vertex AI enterprise customers, the firewall is in place. No training on your content.

Microsoft (Copilot)

Microsoft's consumer Copilot trains on conversations by default for signed-in users, though they de-identify the data first (stripping names, emails, phone numbers). The enterprise products, Microsoft 365 Copilot and Azure OpenAI, are completely walled off from training. The documentation is explicit and unambiguous.

02 / The Personalization Question
Memory, Custom Instructions, and Your Style Preferences
These are for you, not for the model

This is the part that trips people up the most. When ChatGPT "remembers" that you prefer bullet points, or Claude knows your name and your writing tone, it feels like the system is building a dossier on you. Like it is studying you.

But the mechanics are different from what the anxiety suggests. Memory and personalization features operate at the individual account level. They make your next conversation better. They are not scraped and fed into the foundation model that millions of people use. When you tell Claude your style preferences, that information stays in your session context. It is not shipped off to a training data center in Nevada.

OpenAI's Memory feature follows the same training toggle as everything else: if you have opted out of training, your memories are not used for model improvement. Same for custom instructions. Anthropic's memory system was designed from the ground up to be session-scoped and user-controlled. Google's personalization features (like Gemini Gems and saved preferences) operate within the Gemini Apps activity framework and are subject to the same Keep Activity toggle.

So yes, you are right if your instinct says these features exist to give you a better experience, not to creepily surveil you. The data backs that up. But I want to be honest: "the data backs it up" is not the same as "there is zero risk." Because the policies can change, and in Anthropic's case they already did once. More on that tension later.

03 / The Compliance Arms Race
How AI Labs Stack Up Against the Tools You Already Trust
The gap is narrower than you think

Here is where the comparison at the heart of this piece gets genuinely interesting. Because when you line up the security certifications of AI platforms against the traditional software that has been around for decades, the gap is much smaller than most people assume. In some cases, it has disappeared entirely.

I looked at the compliance certifications side by side. The results surprised me.

Microsoft Azure OpenAI inherits Azure's enormous compliance portfolio: over 100 certifications, including SOC 1/2/3, ISO 27001, FedRAMP High, HIPAA, HITRUST, and PCI-DSS. It is, by a wide margin, the most enterprise-ready AI platform on the market.

Google Gemini became the first generative AI assistant to receive independent FedRAMP High authorization in March 2025. It holds the full ISO suite, HIPAA, HITRUST, and PCI-DSS v4.0.

OpenAI now holds SOC 2 Type II, the ISO 27001 family, HIPAA BAA, and CSA STAR Level 1. The main gap is FedRAMP; they are pursuing it but do not yet have independent authorization. Government customers access OpenAI models through Azure's FedRAMP environment instead.

Anthropic maintains SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, ISO 42001, CSA STAR Level 2, and HIPAA BAA. Claude is available for FedRAMP High and DoD IL4/5 workloads through AWS GovCloud.

Now here is where the comparison takes an unexpected turn. Because not all traditional software has the same level of compliance.

Stata holds virtually no cloud security certifications. No SOC 2. No ISO 27001. No HIPAA. No FedRAMP. It is desktop software. Organizations using Stata for sensitive data are responsible for implementing their own security controls. The same is true for SPSS in many deployment configurations. These are deeply trusted tools in academic and research settings, and yet their compliance posture is, objectively, narrower than what the AI labs now offer. That is not a criticism of those tools. It is an observation about how uneven our trust benchmarks can be.

All four AI labs hold ISO 42001, the new standard specifically designed for AI governance and responsible AI management. Traditional enterprise software vendors have not broadly pursued this certification. In this one area, the AI companies are actually ahead.

"The consumer/enterprise divide matters more than the AI/traditional-software divide. A ChatGPT Enterprise deployment with Zero Data Retention and SOC 2 Type II certification offers stronger documented privacy protections than many traditional tools that have been around for decades."

04 / The Uncomfortable History
Traditional Software Has a Documented Track Record Too
And it is not the track record most people expect

I want to be direct about this, because the data here is striking.

Oracle settled a class action lawsuit for $115 million in 2024. The allegation? That the company built a surveillance infrastructure compiling dossiers on over five billion people using cookies, tracking pixels, and offline purchase data. Without consent. Oracle's Data Cloud aggregated data from more than 200 partners, covering 115 million American households. CEO Larry Ellison publicly boasted about the company's massive consumer database at Oracle OpenWorld in 2016.

This is the same company whose database products are a staple of enterprise and academic computing worldwide.

Microsoft has accumulated $1.59 billion in penalties since 2000 across 23 recorded violations. In 2023, the FTC fined them $20 million for illegally collecting children's personal information through Xbox and retaining it for years. A Dutch government investigation in 2018 found Microsoft conducting large-scale, covert data collection through Office applications, including keystroke data, across 300,000 government computers.

This is the same suite of products that serves as the backbone of productivity software in virtually every workplace on the planet.

Dropbox suffered a 2012 breach that exposed 68 million user credentials (discovered publicly in 2016), followed by a 2024 Dropbox Sign breach that compromised emails, usernames, phone numbers, hashed passwords, and API keys for all users of the service.

By comparison, the AI companies' documented incidents are real but smaller in scale. OpenAI's most significant regulatory action was Italy's €15 million GDPR fine in December 2024, related to training data practices and a March 2023 bug that briefly exposed some ChatGPT Plus subscribers' payment information. Researchers also demonstrated that targeted API queries could extract memorized training examples from ChatGPT. Over 100,000 ChatGPT credentials ended up on the dark web, though that was traced to malware on user devices, not to an OpenAI breach.

None of this is zero. But the scale difference is real and worth sitting with. The documented privacy incidents from traditional software companies are, by any objective measure, larger in scope and more frequent than those from AI labs. That does not mean AI companies are flawless. It means the comparison is not as one-sided as the prevailing narrative suggests.

05 / The Trust Gap
Why the Fear Persists Despite the Evidence
It is not fully rational, but it is not fully irrational either

A YouGov survey from December 2025 found that only 5 percent of Americans trust AI "a lot," even though 35 percent use it weekly. A University of Melbourne and KPMG global study of over 48,000 people across 47 countries found that trust in AI has actually declined since 2022, despite far greater exposure. And a Relyance AI survey found that 81 percent of consumers assume AI companies are lying about their privacy practices. Regardless of what those practices actually are.

So what is going on?

Part of it is an opacity problem, and this one is legitimate. When you put data into a traditional database, you have a mental model of where it goes. A table. A row. A server. When data enters an AI model, it undergoes a transformation that is genuinely hard to reason about. The boundary between "stored" and "learned" gets blurry. And researchers have shown that LLMs can sometimes regurgitate memorized content, which means the data is not gone in the way a user might expect.

Part of it is pure novelty. We have normalized decades of data collection by traditional software. Microsoft telemetry, Oracle tracking, Google analytics? Background noise. An AI system "learning from" your conversations triggers a visceral, almost anthropomorphic reaction. It feels more intimate than "storage." The word "learning" does a lot of psychological work.

Part of it is media asymmetry. Italy banning ChatGPT in 2023 was a global headline for weeks. Oracle's $115 million surveillance settlement barely registered outside of tech news.

And part of it is assumed guilt. When four in five people believe AI companies are lying regardless of what the disclosures say, you are dealing with a trust deficit that better policies alone cannot fix. The distrust is operating upstream of the evidence.

"When four in five people believe AI companies are lying regardless of what the disclosures say, you are dealing with a trust deficit that better policies alone cannot fix."

That said, I do not want to be dismissive. The distrust is not entirely without basis. AI systems create genuinely novel risks.

06 / What Is Actually New and Scary
AI-Specific Threats That Traditional Software Does Not Face
Prompt injection, data extraction, and why the paranoid are not entirely wrong

Prompt injection has been ranked the number one LLM security risk by OWASP for three consecutive years, and it is not a bug. It is an architectural limitation. LLMs cannot reliably tell the difference between instructions and data. This has led to real-world exploits: a GitHub Copilot vulnerability in 2025 that enabled remote code execution through poisoned code comments, a hijacked Google Gemini session via a malicious calendar invite, and a Slack AI data exfiltration through knowledge base poisoning.

OWASP's assessment is blunt. Prompt injection will persist as a top vulnerability because true elimination would require fundamental architectural changes to how these models work.

Training data extraction is a demonstrated reality, not a theoretical concern. Researchers showed that roughly $200 worth of API queries could pull thousands of verbatim memorized training examples out of ChatGPT. Related work on embedding inversion demonstrated that vector embeddings, widely assumed to be safe proxies for sensitive text, can sometimes be reversed to reconstruct original sentences.

Data poisoning targets the AI supply chain itself. The PoisonedRAG attack showed that inserting just five malicious documents into a corpus of millions caused an AI system to return attacker-controlled false answers 90 percent of the time for specific queries. That is alarming for any organization using retrieval-augmented generation over internal knowledge bases.

The frontier labs are responding with layered defenses. Anthropic activated its ASL-3 safeguards in May 2025. OpenAI maintains a Preparedness Framework with capability evaluations. Google DeepMind's Frontier Safety Framework uniquely assesses models for deceptive alignment. But the 2025 AI Safety Index from the Future of Life Institute concluded that capabilities are accelerating faster than risk management across the board.

These are real concerns. They deserve attention. But they are engineering problems being actively worked on, not evidence that AI companies are secretly hoarding your data.

The Takeaway
So, Is Your AI Any Different from Your Other Software?

The evidence, when you lay it out side by side, does not support either extreme. AI companies are not recklessly harvesting personal data. Their enterprise tiers offer protections that meet or exceed what traditional software provides. Their compliance certifications have matured at a pace that is, frankly, impressive for companies that are only a few years old. And their documented privacy track record is, by the numbers, cleaner than some of the most established names in enterprise software.

At the same time, the consumer-tier story has real caveats. Free and standard accounts do train on your data by default. The opt-out mechanisms work, but they have exceptions that reward careful policy readers and penalize everyone else. And AI systems face genuinely novel attack surfaces that your Excel spreadsheet will never have to worry about.

The actionable version of all this? Choose the right product tier for the sensitivity of what you are working on. If you are doing research with identifiable data, use enterprise or API tiers with explicit no-training guarantees. If you are brainstorming marketing ideas on the free version of ChatGPT, understand that you can opt out of training in about ten seconds, and you should. And when it comes to the broader question of whether AI tools deserve less trust than the traditional software we have all relied on for years, the evidence suggests the answer is more complicated than a simple yes or no. The more honest question might be: are we applying the same standard of scrutiny to everything, or just to the thing that is new?

The farmer trusted the plow because he had used it for years. The tractor was suspect because it was new. The tractor was also better. Eventually, the farmer figured that out.

We will too.
